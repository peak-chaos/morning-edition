⚡ Urgent · Ban Boring B2B fodder
Fortune 5 April 2026 Corporate comms

The "Storyteller" has become marketing's newest messiah — and the buzzword has already rotted.

This is a Ban Boring B2B layup. Every LinkedIn bio is about to grow a "storyteller" suffix, and the craft it was supposed to stand for — actually saying something true about the brand — has already been replaced by theatre.

Fortune's commentary skewers the way "Storyteller" has been elevated to a near-mystical job title, "spoken of in hushed, awed tones" and gifted with the wisdom of poets, literary giants and comedian-style truth-tellers. The reality on the ground: more decks about "brand mythology", more pastel slides, same generic copy underneath.

For me specifically, this is newsletter content in an afternoon — pull three real job ads with "storyteller" in the title, show the generic launch copy the brand is actually shipping, and let the gap do the roasting. For Go1, the better play is stealth: we don't adopt the word, we adopt the discipline. Our L&D audience can smell a buzzword from the first bullet on slide two.

⚡ Actionable · Rethink the campaign stack
HBR February 2026 AI · B2B strategy
02

AI isn't just changing the work.
It's changing who the buyer is.

Two shifts at once: conversational AI is eating traditional search, and AI agents are starting to make purchasing decisions on behalf of humans. If you're a B2B marketer waiting to "see how it plays out", you're already behind.

HBR frames this as marketing's biggest pivot since mobile: the first front is discovery. People are forming opinions inside AI Overviews and chat assistants — often without ever visiting a website. The second front is acquisition. Agents evaluate, compare and transact on behalf of a decision-maker. That means our case studies, comparison pages and even our pricing need to be machine-legible, structured and trusted — not just pretty.

For Go1, this is a direct line to the L&D buying committee: are our product pages built for an agent doing due diligence at 2am, or for a human browsing a demo form? For my own work as a marketing advisor, this is the whole pitch — generative engine optimisation is the new SEO, and most brands haven't noticed.

Fortune 5 April 2026 Creator economy
03 — a blueprint

Corporate Natalie turned a $500 brand deal into her own agency.

This is the creator-to-consultancy arc I keep circling. She didn't build a production house. She built a room — and she charges brands to be in it before the brief is written.

Expand Co-Lab, her new shop, doesn't represent talent or take commissions. It works with a collective (Brandon Smithwrick, Corporate Bro, Sara Uy, Rachel Tokar among them) to sit with brands during the briefing process — not after the deck has been polished. The premise: most creator briefs are bad because creators weren't in the room when the campaign was imagined.

My Ban Boring B2B audience is exactly this kind of marketer. The insight I can lift for my own newsletter is the economic shift: creators are no longer an execution line item, they're becoming a strategy line item. If I want to do consulting work alongside writing, this is the model — get paid for the taste, not the posts.

No. 04 — A brief in two columns Fast Company · 2026
Fast Company April 2026 Campaign fundamentals

2026 will be the year marketers quietly rediscover the basics.

The thesis is almost embarrassingly simple: after three years of AI-tool adoption whiplash, leaders' biggest 2026 investments are going back into customer experience and brand building — not the newest social platform or a shinier piece of ad tech. Fifty-five percent of marketing decision-makers expect AI to reshape strategy and execution, but the competitive edge is in embedding AI across the lifecycle, not in chasing it.

For me, this is permission to stop apologising for caring about messaging hierarchy, proof points and the unglamorous middle of the funnel. Briefs that start with "what's our actual POV" will beat briefs that start with "can we do a Sora video".

For Go1's L&D buyers — who are overwhelmingly time-poor, under-resourced and asked to prove ROI — "rediscover the basics" isn't a throwback. It's a permission slip. Clear positioning. Clear landing pages. Clear sales enablement. AI is leverage on top of that, not a substitute for it.

My lens: fold this into the next campaign review as the opening slide. "Are we doing the basics well before we do AI?"

What happens when the short-form video bubble pops?

Broderick's argument is that the economics of short-form video — platforms, creators and the brands funding them — are unsustainable, and we're about to find out what survives when the money leaves the room.

The through-line: Sora 2 launched as a TikTok-shaped social app, OpenAI is pushing AI-generated feeds, and brands are pivoting from UGC-style sludge to polished mini-series (Tower 28, Milani) because the "brain rot" middle is being devalued. Short-form isn't dying; it's stratifying. Cheap slop at the bottom, expensive brand films at the top, nothing in the middle.

For me specifically: every "let's do a TikTok series for the campaign" brief should now open with a question about where we sit on that stratification. For my own newsletter audience, there's a great roast in here about how many B2B brands have spent 18 months making bad Reels and learned nothing.

Ad Age April 2026 Forecast

100 ad leaders told Ad Age what they think 2026 actually looks like. Read it like a map.

Across 100 CMOs and agency heads, three patterns repeat. First: AI isn't a tool anymore, it's the operating system — and that separates marketers who embed it across the lifecycle from those who bolted it onto one campaign. Second: creators are replacing the agency model, not supplementing it. Third: brand clarity beats brand volume — the winners "play it safe" only because they're brutally intentional about what they stand for.

My lens: this is the single most useful forecast document for scoping the next 12 months at Go1 and for shaping my Ban Boring B2B editorial calendar. It's also a cheat sheet for the "what should we be doing differently" slide every CMO is asked to present in Q2.

Fortune 17 April 2026 Workplace culture
7%
of employees today would quit over a mandatory RTO — down from 51% in January 2025.

The power has swung back to employers. The vibe of work has changed with it.

In less than two years, the bargaining gravity has flipped. The Great Resignation is now the Great Compliance — and for anyone doing employer-brand work, the tone you used in 2024 will read as smug by June.

Fortune's reporting is blunt: flexibility is being traded for stability, benefits are shrinking, and leverage has shifted toward the employer. But there's a long-tail counter-trend — as Millennial and Gen Z managers ascend, they're reopening remote and hybrid work on their own teams. The pendulum is swinging back; it's just wearing a different suit.

For Go1, this is a direct signal for L&D narrative: employees are more anxious, less mobile, and more open to upskilling as a hedge. For my own work, it's the setup for a punchy "corporate culture in 2026" essay — the tone brands use in hiring ads versus the reality of what's actually happening to the worker.

Ad Age April 2026 LinkedIn · B2B
08

B2B brands are pouring money into LinkedIn creators. Quietly. Deliberately.

If you're running B2B campaigns and haven't rebuilt your media mix to include creator partnerships on LinkedIn, you're missing the platform's fastest-growing ad surface. LinkedIn's first NewFronts positioned it as a "video-first" platform — with pre-roll ads on creator-hosted shows.

Ad Age reports B2B marketers are spearheading creator economy growth on LinkedIn: pre-roll on creator shows, branded video partnerships, and a wave of B2C brands quietly testing the platform now that the tooling is credible. Disclosure practices are still sloppy — worth watching for backlash — but the fundamentals are shifting fast.

For Go1: the obvious integration is partnering with mid-tier L&D and HR creators who already have trust with our buyer, rather than pushing more owned-channel content into a saturated feed. For my Ban Boring B2B newsletter, this is my home turf — the creator content Go1 *should* be making is also the content my audience is starving for.

Brand voice · Cautionary tale
Fortune 1 April 2026 Corporate comms · RTO
09

Jamie Dimon says his anti-remote culture "would crush you." Top talent is begging to differ.

This is a brand-voice-gone-feral case study. A Fortune 500 CEO telling his workforce they'd be "crushed" is not a leadership quote. It's a recruiting ad for his competitors. Read it as a masterclass in how not to write internal comms that inevitably become external.

Dimon doubled down on JPMorgan's RTO mandate with a line that instantly went viral in exactly the wrong rooms. The follow-up reporting from Fortune is damning: top-tier talent is openly skeptical, and other banks are quietly using the quote as a pitch for their hybrid flexibility. Amazon-style full-RTO pushes keep running into practical walls — not enough desks, not enough will.

For me: this is perfect Ban Boring B2B material about brand voice consistency — what you say about your culture and what your culture actually feels like are now audited in real-time by every employee with a phone. For Go1, this is the opening of a narrative campaign: "Your people are learning whether you like it or not. The question is from whom."

Fast Company April 2026 Women & work
And finally —

Women leaders are ditching the old workplace rulebook — and winning because of it.

Against a backdrop of slowing CEO progress and a still-broken first rung to management, the women actually making it through are doing so by refusing to perform the old playbook. The pace of change is "stubbornly slow", but the style of change is accelerating.

Fast Company's piece profiles women leaders rejecting rigid hierarchy, relentless visibility theatre and 24/7 availability — and outperforming. Fortune's parallel reporting notes that only 7 more women will hold Fortune 500 CEO roles in Q1 2026 (including Meg O'Neill at BP, the first woman to ever lead a Big Oil firm), while women's board seat growth saw its smallest uptick in a decade. The progress is real and fragile at once.

For my own lens: there's a quieter through-line here about tone of voice in leadership comms. The women getting ahead aren't mimicking the "crush you" school — they're building brand voices that are direct, warm, and refuse to confuse volume with authority. That's an editorial I want to write.
